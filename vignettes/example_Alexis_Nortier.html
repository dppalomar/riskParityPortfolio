This note contains an example under consideration by Alexis Nortier.

Risk-parity portfolio formulation

The risk-parity portfolio formulation is of the form [1][2]: \[\begin{array}{ll} \underset{\mathbf{w}}{\textsf{minimize}} & R(\mathbf{w})\\ \textsf{subject to} & \mathbf{1}^T\mathbf{w}=1\\ & \mathbf{w}\ge\mathbf{0}, \end{array}\] where the risk term is of the form (double summation) \(R(\mathbf{w}) = \sum_{i,j=1}^{N}(g_{ij}(\mathbf{w}))^{2}\) or simply (single summation) \(R(\mathbf{w}) = \sum_{i=1}^{N}(g_{i}(\mathbf{w}))^{2}\).

Parameter definition for the test

Consider the following parameters: \[\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1.0000 & 0.0015 & -0.0119\\ 0.0015 & 1.0000 & -0.0308\\ -0.0119 & -0.0308 & 1.0000 \end{array} \right]\] \[\mathbf{b} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0.1594 \\ 0.0126 \\ 0.8280 \end{array} \right]\] and the corresponding code:

Sigma <- rbind(c(1.0000, 0.0015, -0.0119),
               c(0.0015, 1.0000, -0.0308),
               c(-0.0119, -0.0308, 1.0000))
b <- c(0.1594, 0.0126, 0.8280)

The optimal solution is known to be \[\mathbf{w}^\star = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0.2799 \\ 0.0877 \\ 0.6324 \end{array} \right],\] but the Matlab implementation seems to converge to \[\mathbf{w} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0.3106 \\ 0.0000 \\ 0.6895 \end{array} \right].\]

Formulation rc double-index

Consider the risk expression: \[R(\mathbf{w}) = \sum_{i,j=1}^{N}\left(w_{i}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}\right)_{i}-w_{j}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}\right)_{j}\right)^{2}.\]

library(xts)
library(riskParityPortfolio)

res_gen_solver <- riskParityPortfolioGenSolver(Sigma, formulation = "rc-double-index")
res_sca <- riskParityPortfolioSCA(Sigma, formulation = "rc-double-index")
res_gen_solver$w
#> [1] 0.3304543 0.3336630 0.3358827
res_sca$w
#> [1] 0.3304546 0.3336630 0.3358824

Formulation rc-over-var vs b

Consider the risk expression: \[R(\mathbf{w}) = \sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(\frac{w_{i}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}\right)_i}{\mathbf{w}^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}}-b_i\right)^{2}.\] The general solver seems to be too sensitive to the initial point in this formulation:

res_gen_solver1 <- riskParityPortfolioGenSolver(Sigma, b, formulation = "rc-over-var-vs-b")
res_gen_solver1$w
#> [1] 0.27986280 0.08774909 0.63238811
last(res_gen_solver1$obj_fun)
#> [1] 1.018771e-22
res_gen_solver2 <- riskParityPortfolioGenSolver(Sigma, b, w0 = b, formulation = "rc-over-var-vs-b")
res_gen_solver2$w
#> [1]  3.105589e-01 -2.445212e-19  6.894411e-01
last(res_gen_solver2$obj_fun)
#> [1] 0.00023814
res_gen_solver3 <- riskParityPortfolioGenSolver(Sigma, b, w0 = c(1, 1, 1)/3, formulation = "rc-over-var-vs-b")
res_gen_solver3$w
#> [1] -1.110223e-16  9.992007e-16  1.000000e+00
last(res_gen_solver3$obj_fun)
#> [1] 0.05515112
barplot(rbind(res_gen_solver1$w, res_gen_solver2$w, res_gen_solver3$w),
        main = "Portfolios with different initial points", xlab = "stocks", ylab = "dollars", beside = TRUE, 
        col = rainbow8equal[1:3])

Formulation rc-over-sd vs b-times-sd

Consider the risk expression: \[R(\mathbf{w}) = \sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(\frac{w_{i}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}\right)_i}{\sqrt{\mathbf{w}^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}}}-b_i\sqrt{\mathbf{w}^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}}\right)^{2} = \sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(\frac{r_i}{\sqrt{\mathbf{1}^T\mathbf{r}}}-b_i\sqrt{\mathbf{1}^T\mathbf{r}}\right)^{2}.\]

The general solver seems to be more robust with this formulation:

res_gen_solver1 <- riskParityPortfolioGenSolver(Sigma, b, formulation = "rc-over-sd-vs-b-times-sd")
res_gen_solver1$w

#> [1] 2.761566e-16

#> [1] 0.27986274 0.08774923 0.63238803
last(res_gen_solver2$obj_fun)
#> [1] 1.913439e-15
res_gen_solver3 <- riskParityPortfolioGenSolver(Sigma, b, w0 = c(1, 1, 1)/3, formulation = "rc-over-sd-vs-b-times-sd")

Conclusion

The risk-parity portfolio problem is highly nonconvex so any numerical method can in principle give a suboptimal solution. From the experiments above, it seems that both methods (based on a general nonlinear solver and on the SCA [1]) give the correct solution if initialized with a proper initial point, but otherwise may give a suboptimal solution. Also, it seems that the formulation rc-over-sd vs b-times-sd gives more stable solutions than the formulation rc-over-var vs b. In any case, all these conclusions are only based on the numerical experiments above and more exhaustive tests should be performed.
